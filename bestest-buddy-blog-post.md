# Bestest Buddy: an Obsidian plugin that acts more like a companion than a chatbot

Most AI tools for writing start from the same premise: open a chat box, ask for help, and let the model take over a large chunk of the interaction. Bestest Buddy is built from a different instinct. Instead of turning Obsidian into another assistant workspace, it adds a small presence to the edge of your vault: a persistent buddy that lives in the sidebar, reacts to how you write, and occasionally says exactly one useful thing.

That difference matters more than it sounds. Writing often benefits from a little friction, a little atmosphere, and a little witness. It does not always benefit from being pulled into a full conversation. Bestest Buddy is designed for people who want their notes to stay notes. The plugin keeps the center of gravity inside the document while still giving the workspace a sense of life.

At the heart of the plugin is the idea that each Obsidian vault should feel distinct. Bestest Buddy generates a vault-scoped companion with its own identity and keeps that identity stable over time. Your buddy has a name, a personality, a species, a rarity, visual traits, and a set of stats that shape how it reacts. It is not a disposable prompt result. It is meant to feel like something your vault hatched and kept.

That identity shows up visually in a dedicated sidebar panel. The buddy appears as an animated ASCII creature with a compact interface around it: status indicators, a speech bubble, small controls, and a direct input when you want to ask for help. The presentation is intentionally lightweight. It is playful without becoming noisy, and it fits the texture of Obsidian better than a full assistant dashboard would.

The part I find most interesting is how the plugin pays attention. Bestest Buddy watches for patterns in your writing session: opening notes, starting fresh pages, writing in bursts, revising heavily, drifting into long pauses, and returning after a break. From those events it derives a rough sense of mood and session state. That gives the companion enough context to react in ways that feel timely without pretending to be all-knowing.

The reactions are short on purpose. Bestest Buddy is not trying to produce paragraphs of advice every time you hesitate. It is aiming for the kind of line that can nudge you back into motion, quietly acknowledge what your draft is doing, or make the workspace feel a bit less sterile. Sometimes that means noticing momentum. Sometimes it means observing that you are deep in revision. Sometimes it just means greeting a new note like a blank page with possibility instead of pressure.

There is also a direct mode when you want to address the buddy yourself. You can send a short message from the panel or ask about the current note, and the plugin can ground its reply in your active selection or the note itself. Even there, the design stays disciplined. The reply is supposed to be compact, in character, and helpful without turning into a sprawling assistant monologue. That boundary is one of the project’s strongest ideas.

Another good choice is that the plugin remains useful even when external model calls are unavailable. It can use hosted language models for hatching a richer personality and generating more expressive reactions, but it also has local fallback behavior when no API key is configured or a request fails. In practice, that means the companion concept is not held hostage by network access. The plugin still has a shape and a voice, even in a degraded mode.

Bestest Buddy also leaves room for personalization without losing the core premise. You can tune how talkative it is, whether it includes note context, whether the sidebar runs in a minimal mode, and how sharp its tone should be. You can customize visual traits and stats as well. That flexibility matters because the line between “companionable” and “annoying” is narrow. A good ambient tool should adapt to the temperament of the person using it.

What makes the project stand out is restraint. Bestest Buddy does not try to autonomously rewrite your notes. It does not try to become a second editor pane full of synthetic prose. It does not frame every writing session as a request for optimization. Instead, it explores a smaller and arguably more interesting question: what if an AI writing tool acted less like an omnipresent assistant and more like a weird little creature that learns the rhythm of your vault?

That is a much more specific product thesis, and a better one. Obsidian users tend to care about environment, ritual, and the feel of a workspace. Bestest Buddy meets them there. It adds personality without demanding attention, context without surveillance theater, and interaction without turning every note into a chat session. It feels closer to software companionship than software automation.

If I were describing the plugin in one sentence, I would say this: Bestest Buddy gives your vault a persistent writing companion that notices your habits, reacts with brevity, and makes Obsidian feel a little more inhabited. In a landscape full of AI tools trying to do more, its strength is knowing exactly how much to do and then stopping there.
